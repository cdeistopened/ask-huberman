Tell us about mitochondria in the framework of mental health.

Most people know mitochondria as the powerhouse of the cell, if they know them at all. These tiny little organelles, and the "powerhouse of the cell" reference means that mitochondria take the breakdown products of the food that we're eating. They are the primary thing using the oxygen that we're breathing in. They are creating the carbon dioxide that we're breathing out, and they are turning food into ATP, which is the energy currency of the cell. They're taking food and oxygen and lots of other things—but let's just simplify to food and oxygen—converting it into ATP, and that is what the powerhouse of the cell refers to. 

There is no doubt they do that. There is no doubt that when that process stops, humans have about six minutes or so and then we're dead. That process is critical to life. There is no other process in the body that you can disrupt that will kill the organism faster. It is central to living organisms, this production of ATP. So I don't at all mean to take away or minimize that function, but research over the last 25 years has completely upended that simplistic notion of what mitochondria are doing. They are actually doing so much more. 

Some people have created the reference that mitochondria are like the workers inside a cell. In order for a cell to work, you need a workforce because there's so much that needs to be done: signals need to be sent, all these different things need to be functioning. Mitochondria are absolutely providing the energy for those things to happen, but they're also orchestrating a lot of it. For example, they play a direct role in converting food into some of the substrates for the production of neurotransmitters, but they also go further. They store some neurotransmitters, like GABA, within themselves, and that plays a role in GABA's release from a neuron. They actually go to the cell membrane and move along the membrane dispensing vesicles of neurotransmitters. When you take the mitochondria away from the synapse but provide that synapse with ATP, vesicles don't get released. Neurotransmitters aren't getting released. The mitochondria are doing more. We don't exactly know what, but they're doing more than just providing the energy. 

They play a role in turning inflammation and immune cells both on and off. They help start the process, but they also help coordinate the cessation of the process. They play an instrumental role in both the first and the last step in the synthesis of cortisol, and they play a role in the first step in the synthesis of all of the steroid hormones, which include estrogen, testosterone, and progesterone. If you have dysregulation of cortisol, or if you have dysregulation of testosterone, estrogen, or progesterone, you must understand the role of mitochondria in that dysregulation because they are critical in the production and release of these hormones. 

They are the primary regulator of epigenetics. Epigenetics are the expression of genes from the cell nucleus, and researchers have long known that that's related to levels of reactive oxygen species, levels of calcium, and other cell signals. Those cell signals are mostly originating within mitochondria. During the development of any cell, mitochondria—they are like a universe unto themselves, and there's so much we don't know about them—but what researchers have found is that mitochondria actually line up, literally line up in an organized fashion around the cell nucleus and take on different conformations. That is somehow sending signals to the genes to result in the expression or the suppression of different genes from the nucleus. When researchers take these mitochondria and mess them up, the cell doesn't develop normally. 

They've been implicated in all of the phases of the human stress response to psychological stress. That includes cortisol release, noradrenaline release, inflammation, and epigenetic changes. Those are the four buckets of the human stress response: cortisol, adrenaline, inflammation, and epigenetic changes. Researchers actually manipulated mitochondrial genes—two genes in the cell nucleus that control for mitochondrial proteins and two genes in mitochondria themselves. By manipulating these four different genes one at a time in mice, they could impact all four aspects of the stress response. What that means is that mitochondria are somehow involved in regulating the human stress response. 

The way that I think about it, and the way that many researchers actually think about it now, is that mitochondria—there are hundreds, sometimes thousands of them in each of our cells; most neurons have thousands of mitochondria—the mitochondria are actually moving around. They use the cytoskeleton to move around the cell. They fuse with each other; it's called mitochondrial dynamics. They change shape, they do all sorts of things, and again, that impacts all of these signaling processes. But that's just within one little cell. You can think of one cell as almost a village of mitochondria that are all just doing different things and working together to help that cell function. 

In fact, when you think about hormones like cortisol, you can think about it as a way for mitochondria in one cell to produce cortisol that gets sent to mitochondria in another cell to make that other cell do something—to either increase its activity or decrease its activity. Some people actually think about human cells as just a network of mitochondria. All the mitochondria throughout the body and brain are just doing all sorts of things. At the end of the day, we come back to common sense. At the end of the day, it's about helping the organism adapt and survive. Ultimately, organism rule number one: they need to survive. Rule number two: they need to reproduce. And rule number three: they need to adapt. Mitochondria are playing a foundational role in all of those basic aspects of organismal survival.
