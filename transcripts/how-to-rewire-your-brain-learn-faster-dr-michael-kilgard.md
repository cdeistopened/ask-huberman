## Neuromodulators: The Brain's Chemical Drivers

Let's talk about serotonin, dopamine, norepinephrine, and acetylcholine. These are neuromodulators. You mentioned the hubs in the brain that release them.

[84:00]
- They remind me of the four fundamental forces of nature. We're looking for the grand unified theory. What do they do individually, and how do they work together? My story began with an experiment by Gregg Recanzone. A monkey has a vibration on his finger, and if he pays attention, he can quadruple the neurons that respond to that touch.

[85:00]
The monkey was also listening to a sound, but his auditory cortex didn't change because he wasn't listening to the tone. One monkey learned touch; another learned sound.

[85:30]
I thought, "How does a single cell know what I'm paying attention to?" The closest thing was acetylcholine. If you block acetylcholine, you won't remember anything.

[87:00]
Anticholinergics like scopolamine are amnestics. We knew acetylcholine was responsible, but not how it worked. I hypothesized that every time an animal focuses, there's a burst of acetylcholine.

[87:30]
I put an electrode in those neurons and activated them while a tone happened. I was able to quadruple the neurons that respond to that tone, even though it meant nothing to the animal.

[88:00]
The brain can be changed based on the relative timing of the release of neurotransmitters. It requires spaced repetition.

[88:30]

[90:00]

[90:30]

[91:30]

[92:00]
