I was interested in psychology. I was an avid reader. As a matter of fact, I embarrassed my father, or actually made him disappointed, when for a birthday—I think I was 14 years old—I asked him to buy me the 12 volumes of Freud's writings. Of course, I couldn't even understand half of what these books had in them. I was very interested in many things, and I must say to you that my interest in science, in particular physics, doesn't come from the standard thing that you see here in the United States—namely, I was not a whiz kid in math. I was not one of these people that can do things very quickly. But I was interested because I thought that physics was going to complement my attempt at understanding how the whole universe is put together. 

The philosophers were saying all sorts of things. I went to a very special school where I learned six years of Latin. I had to read things like Cicero and cosmogonies that really didn't mean much to me, but suddenly I started discovering that physics might be interesting. I had a cousin, Hector, who was a physicist—a particle physicist already. He was living at that time in France, so there was a little bit of an influence. 

But my interest was in things that had to do with fairly abstract ideas. I cannot believe that at one point or the other I was very good in geometry class, being able to prove theorems. The teacher just said, "Let's prove this," and I was somehow able to reason through and come to some proofs. I think that I was very interested in ideas and not necessarily in the very concrete aspects of science at that time.
