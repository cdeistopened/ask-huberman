[9:00]
Stevens and his colleagues were looking for anesthetics that would have fewer side effects than PCP, which is phencyclidine. Now PCP had already been developed for use as an anesthetic, but PCP had a number of really terrible side effects, including extended delirium, extended meaning a delirium that would go on for many, many hours or even days after people came out of anesthesia. It also produced severe psychotic episodes,

[9:30]

So ketamine was developed as an alternative, and indeed ketamine produces much less delirium and much less psychotic symptomology than PCP. Ketamine was approved by the FDA as an anesthetic in 1970 and was used widely in the Vietnam War for surgical anesthesia.

So let's talk about the mechanism of action of ketamine. How does ketamine actually produce its effects? Ketamine works by blocking what's called the NMDA receptor, which is the N-methyl-D-aspartate receptor. Now, as I mentioned earlier, the NMDA receptor is a glutamate receptor. Glutamate is the major excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain.

[14:00]
And when glutamate binds to the NMDA receptor, it opens up a channel that allows ions, charged ions like calcium, to enter the neuron and change the electrical potential of that neuron. And that's the basis of excitatory neurotransmission in the brain. So what ketamine does is it blocks that channel. It's actually what's called an open channel blocker or an uncompetitive antagonist,

[14:30]
meaning it gets into the channel when the channel is open and prevents ions from flowing through. And by doing that, ketamine reduces the amount of excitatory transmission in certain neural circuits. But here's where it gets interesting and perhaps a bit counterintuitive. You might think, well, if you're reducing excitatory transmission, then you would reduce overall brain activity, and that would just make people sedated or unconscious.

[15:00]
But that's not exactly what happens with ketamine at subanesthetic doses. What happens is that ketamine preferentially blocks NMDA receptors on a particular type of neuron called an interneuron. Interneurons are inhibitory neurons. They release the neurotransmitter GABA, which is gamma-aminobutyric acid, and GABA is the main inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brain. So when you block the excitatory input

[15:30]
onto inhibitory interneurons, you essentially reduce inhibition. And when you reduce inhibition, what happens? You get a net increase in excitation elsewhere in the circuit. This is called disinhibition. So ketamine, by blocking NMDA receptors on inhibitory interneurons, causes disinhibition, and that disinhibition leads to increased glutamate release on other neurons,

[16:00]
particularly on pyramidal neurons in the prefrontal cortex. And that increased glutamate release activates what are called AMPA receptors, which are another type of glutamate receptor. And when AMPA receptors are activated, that triggers a whole cascade of downstream signaling events, including the release of brain-derived neurotrophic factor, or BDNF.

[16:30]
BDNF is a growth factor that promotes the growth and strengthening of synapses. And so through this mechanism of disinhibition followed by increased glutamate release followed by BDNF release, ketamine triggers synaptic plasticity, the strengthening and growth of synapses, particularly in the prefrontal cortex, which is a brain area that's critically involved in mood regulation.

[17:00]
And that's believed to be the mechanism by which ketamine produces its rapid antidepressant effects. It's not just about the acute subjective experience while you're under the influence of ketamine. It's about the downstream changes in neural circuitry that occur in the hours and days after ketamine administration. And that's a really key concept to understand.
